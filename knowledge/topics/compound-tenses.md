# Compound Tenses

## What This Is
Tenses using haber + past participle: pluperfect, future perfect, and conditional perfect.

## Prerequisites
- present-perfect
- past-imperfect

## Teaching Approach

### The Pattern
"You already know 'he hablado' (I have spoken). Now just use OTHER forms of haber:"

| Tense | Haber Form | Example | Meaning |
|-------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Present perfect | he/has/ha/hemos/han | He comido | I have eaten |
| Pluperfect | había/habías/había/habíamos/habían | Había comido | I had eaten |
| Future perfect | habré/habrás/habrá/habremos/habrán | Habré comido | I will have eaten |
| Conditional perfect | habría/habrías/habría/habríamos/habrían | Habría comido | I would have eaten |

### Pluperfect (Most Important)
"The 'before the past' tense. Something that happened BEFORE another past event."
- "Cuando llegué, ella ya había salido." (When I arrived, she had already left.)
- Había = imperfect of haber. That's it.

### Future Perfect
"Something that will have happened by a certain point."
- "Para las tres, habré terminado." (By 3, I will have finished.)
- Also used for speculation: "Habrá salido ya." (She will have left by now / She's probably left.)

### Conditional Perfect
"Something that would have happened (but didn't)."
- "Habría ido, pero estaba enfermo." (I would have gone, but I was sick.)
- Used in → conditional-subjunctive for past hypotheticals

### Key Teaching Point
"The participle never changes. Haber does all the work. Learn the forms of haber and you have four tenses for free."

## Common Pitfalls
- Changing the participle to match gender/number (NEVER — "ella había comido" not "comida")
- Putting words between haber and participle ("había no comido" — wrong; "no había comido")
- Confusing habría (conditional) with había (imperfect) — listen to the accent

## Connection to Other Concepts
- Conditional perfect is half of → conditional-subjunctive (past hypotheticals: "si hubiera sabido, habría ido")
